## The "Harness" Problem: Why Cloud AI Integrations Are Your Weakest Link

I've been trying to use Copilot in Outlook all week… again. And it's broken… again.

It got me thinking about a fundamental flaw in how we're integrating AI into our workflows right now.

### When the wrapper breaks, the model is useless

The AI industry calls it an integration layer. I call it the harness — the software wrapper that connects a powerful model to your daily tools. The problem is simple: when the harness breaks, all that intelligence disappears from your workflow.

In cloud-heavy, deeply integrated environments like Outlook, Word, or GitHub Copilot, if the vendor's infrastructure fails anywhere, you don't just lose the AI features — you lose your entire workflow. The harness is your weakest link and your vendor's moat at the same time.

This isn't theoretical. Recent outages in GitHub Copilot's backend infrastructure have shown how quickly cloud-hijacked tools can go dark. When the API goes down or authentication tokens are rejected, enterprise pipelines stall and productivity hits a hard stop.

### What happens when the AI reverts to nothing

If the harness fails, your AI assistant goes from indispensable to invisible overnight. You've built your workflow around it, trained your team on it, and structured your day around it — then it vanishes behind an error message you can't fix.

By the time the vendor patches the outage, hours are gone. And if it happens repeatedly, trust in the tool erodes faster than any roadmap can rebuild it.

### Why I'm pushing for leaner, local stacks

I've been moving toward locally deployed solutions, and the case keeps getting stronger:

**Reliability.** Local, zero-trust stacks don't depend on third-party API availability or corporate firewall throttling. Your tools work when your machine works.

**Resilience.** If one tool breaks, your data and context stay safe on-prem. You're not locked out of your own information while waiting for someone else to fix their servers.

**Speed and control.** It's not a question of whether these integrations will break, but when. With a local stack, we control the deployment. When things break, we iterate and patch instantly rather than waiting for a vendor's roadmap.

### Where this needs to go

We need to stop treating AI as just another SaaS plugin and start building robust, independent systems that actually serve the user, not the other way around.

The models themselves are getting better every week. The real bottleneck isn't intelligence anymore — it's the delivery layer between that intelligence and the person who needs it. If we want AI to be truly useful, we need to make it resilient.

And resilience starts with owning your stack.
