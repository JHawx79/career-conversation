# Before Judging AI, Judge the Infrastructure

Before we form opinions about AI based on abstract fears, it's worth looking at what it actually looks like in the real world. Data centers are physical buildings you can drive past — concrete, steel, and transformer stations on the edge of town. Because they're visible, they become the thing people pin all their uncertainty about AI onto. A friend told me this month they simply don't know why we even need so many data centers. In context, what they really meant was: do we even need all this AI? That question is worth sitting with, but the answer starts with understanding the infrastructure itself.

Data centers are the factories of the digital age, and their scale is staggering. In the U.S. alone, the planned data center pipeline exceeds hundreds of gigawatts of electrical load ([Baker Tilly](https://www.bakertilly.com/insights/data-center-impact-on-local-communities)). This infrastructure drives real economic value, contributing $162.7 billion in local tax revenue in 2023 alone—a 146% jump from 2017 ([U.S. Chamber of Commerce](https://www.uschamber.com/assets/documents/DataCenters_EconomicBenefits.pdf)).

Rather than ignoring resource concerns, the industry is engineering solutions: Microsoft's 2024 AI data centers now use chip-level cooling to eliminate water evaporation for cooling entirely ([Microsoft Cloud Blog](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-cloud/blog/2024/12/09/sustainable-by-design-next-generation-datacenters-consume-zero-water-for-cooling/)), while cold-climate facilities are capturing waste heat to warm nearby schools and homes ([MinnPost](https://www.minnpost.com/community-voices/2025/11/when-done-right-data-centers-can-be-community-assets/)). When communities negotiate upfront, the model works: Lancaster, Pennsylvania secured a Community Benefits Agreement requiring 100% renewable energy, capping daily water use at 20,000 gallons, and guaranteeing a $10 million direct donation to the city ([Tennessee Lookout](https://tennesseelookout.com/2026/06/26/heres-what-communities-can-do-when-data-centers-arrive/)).

Even infrastructure development creates broader social benefits, like Meta's rural internet expansions in Alabama that improved broadband for surrounding residents ([Gresham Smith](https://www.greshamsmith.com/news-and-insights/data-centers-the-art-of-being-a-good-neighbor-ten-steps-for-better-community-engagement/)). AI isn't just code on a screen; it's high-voltage grids, advanced cooling systems, and construction jobs. Before judging the technology, judge the engineering and the economic reality it's building—one tangible data center at a time.
